# Understanding Jeju's Five-Day Markets (Owe-il-jang)

## Summary

Jeju's traditional five-day markets, or owe-il-jang, rotate through towns across the island on a fixed five-day cycle — a market system that predates modern retail and still structures how many residents shop for produce, seafood, and household goods. The same market does not run daily; each town has its own day within the five-day rotation.

## Why It Matters

For a traveler used to markets or supermarkets open every day, the rotating schedule itself is a piece of cultural information worth understanding — it reflects an older, pre-supermarket rhythm of commerce that Jeju has kept even as convenience retail has grown elsewhere on the island. Knowing how the system works is what makes it usable, rather than a confusing dead end when a market turns out to be closed.

## Local Experience Angle

Markets combine fresh produce and seafood vendors — many selling what they grew or caught themselves — with prepared-food stalls and household goods, and in larger locations such as Jeju-si, secondhand and craft sections as well. For long-stay visitors in particular, following the market rotation across nearby towns is one of the most direct ways to participate in the same weekly routine many local households follow, rather than shopping exclusively at convenience stores or supermarkets.
