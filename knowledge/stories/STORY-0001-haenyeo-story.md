# Voices from the Sea: A Haenyeo's Daily Dive

## Summary

Before sunrise in coastal villages like Sehwa-ri, haenyeo — Jeju's traditional women divers — gather at the "bulteok," a communal spot once used to warm up around a fire before and after diving, now more often a simple shelter for changing and resting between dives. They dive without oxygen tanks, holding their breath for a minute or more at a time to gather abalone, conch, sea urchin, and seaweed from the rocky seafloor. Surfacing is marked by "sumbisori," a distinctive whistled exhale that lets divers track each other's location and safety without speaking. A working day can mean several hours in the water, repeated dive after dive, in a rhythm most haenyeo learned from their mothers rather than in any classroom.

## Why It Matters

Haenyeo culture was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2016, recognizing both the diving practice itself and the distinct matrifocal community structure it produced in Jeju's coastal villages, where women were historically the primary household earners. The number of active haenyeo has declined sharply over recent decades as the practice has aged out of younger generations, making the tradition increasingly rare to witness firsthand rather than through a museum exhibit.

## Local Experience Angle

For travelers, understanding haenyeo culture is far more meaningful when it moves beyond a plaque or a photograph. A structured visit — such as a coastal walk paired with a community museum (see `EXP-0002`) — offers a respectful way to encounter this living tradition without treating working divers as a photo opportunity. Timing and etiquette matter: haenyeo are working, not performing, and visitors should approach their presence with the same respect owed to any laborer at their job.
