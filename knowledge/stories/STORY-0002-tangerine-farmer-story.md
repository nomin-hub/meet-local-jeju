# A Season in the Orchard: A Jeju Tangerine Farmer's Year

## Summary

For many farming families around Seogwipo, the tangerine (gamgyul) year follows a steady rhythm: spring blossoms scent the orchard rows in white, summer brings irrigation and pest management as the fruit sets and swells, and by autumn the green skins begin turning to orange (see `SEASON-0001`). Winter is the culmination — weeks of hand-harvesting, sorting, and packing that determine much of the household's income for the year. It is physical, repetitive work, done tree by tree, often by multiple generations of the same family working side by side.

## Why It Matters

Citrus farming has shaped Jeju's rural economy since the mid-20th century, when many households shifted from subsistence grain farming — historically bounded by the island's stone field walls (see `CULTURE-0001`) — toward tangerines as a cash crop better suited to Jeju's volcanic soil and mild climate. For many families, the orchard is not a backdrop to island life; it is the household's primary livelihood, and the harvest season is the most demanding and important stretch of their year.
