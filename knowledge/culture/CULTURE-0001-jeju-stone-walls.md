# Batdam: The Black Stone Walls of Jeju

## Summary

Batdam are the low, dry-stacked walls of black volcanic basalt that crisscross Jeju's agricultural land, built without mortar from the porous rock that covers much of the island. They mark the boundaries of small, irregularly shaped field plots, a layout shaped by Jeju's volcanic terrain rather than the grid-like field divisions common elsewhere. The same dry-stone building technique also appears around traditional homes and, in coastal villages, sheltering small plots and buildings from the wind.

## Why It Matters

Batdam are functional, not decorative: their loosely stacked, unmortared construction lets Jeju's persistent coastal winds pass through the gaps rather than pushing the wall over, a design solution suited specifically to the island's climate. Jeju's traditional agricultural field system, organized around these stone walls, has been recognized as a Globally Important Agricultural Heritage System by the FAO, reflecting its significance as a long-standing, still-functioning land-management practice rather than a historical relic.

## Local Experience Angle

Batdam are easy to overlook as background scenery, but once recognized, they appear almost everywhere across Jeju's mid-mountain farmland — including around citrus orchards (see `SEASON-0001`, `STORY-0002`) and coastal fishing villages (see `LOCAL-0002`). Pointing them out and explaining their purpose turns an ordinary rural view into a piece of legible cultural and environmental history for a traveler.
